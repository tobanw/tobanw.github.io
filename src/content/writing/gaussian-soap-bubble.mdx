My favorite stats fun fact is that high-dimensional Gaussian distributions have almost all of their probability mass concentrated in a thin shell centered on the mean, like a soap bubble, even though the density function is still a bell-curve with its mode at the center of the bubble.

To see it, draw a point from a standard Gaussian in $d$ dimensions:

$$
X = (X_1,\ldots,X_d), \qquad X_i \sim N(0,1) \text{ independently.}
$$

This is the standard multivariate Gaussian, usually written $X \sim N(0,I_d)$: independent standard-normal coordinates give a jointly normal vector with mean zero and identity covariance.

The point is not usually close to the origin. Its typical distance is about $\sqrt d$. The surprising part is about the density of the distance from the mean: the pointwise Gaussian density is highest at the origin and decreases as you move outward, but the radial density of $R=\lVert X\rVert$ is not decreasing near zero. In high dimensions it rises until about $\sqrt d$, because thin shells farther from the origin contain much more volume. Most of the mass sits in a relatively thin shell, like a soap bubble around the origin.

The proof is a trivial application of the central limit theorem. Let

$$
R = \lVert X \rVert, \qquad \text{so} \qquad R^2 = \sum_{i=1}^d X_i^2.
$$

This is a sum of $d$ iid terms, so the central limit theorem says $R^2$ is approximately normal, centered around $d$, with fluctuations of order $\sqrt d$. Equivalently, $R^2/d$ has relative fluctuations of order $1/\sqrt d$. In short,

$$
R^2 = d + O_p(\sqrt d),
$$

and therefore

$$
R = \sqrt d + O_p(1).
$$

That is the soap bubble phenomenon: $R$ concentrates near $\sqrt d$. There are two different senses of "thin" here. In raw Euclidean distance, a fixed-probability band around $R$ has constant-order width, since $R=\sqrt d+O_p(1)$. But after normalizing by the typical radius, $R/\sqrt d$ concentrates near $1$, with fluctuations of order $1/\sqrt d$. So the shell gets arbitrarily thin relative to its radius, which is the usual concentration-of-measure sense.

This is one of the reasons high-dimensional probability can feel geometrically strange. In one or two dimensions, "near the origin" feels like a plausible place for Gaussian mass to be. In many dimensions, the origin is still the point of highest density, but there is almost no volume there.

The tension is between pointwise density and available volume. At radius $r$, the Gaussian density contributes a factor proportional to $\exp(-r^2/2)$, which pulls mass inward. But the amount of space in a thin spherical shell at radius $r$ grows like $r^{d-1}$, which pushes mass outward. The radial distribution is roughly the product $r^{d-1}\exp(-r^2/2)$. Near the origin, the volume factor pulls this product down to zero; moving outward increases the available volume fast enough that the radial density rises. Past about $\sqrt d$, the exponential density decay dominates and the radial density falls. The shell appears where those two forces balance, right around radius $\sqrt d$. This is a concrete example of concentration of measure: in high dimensions, probability mass often piles up in a narrow region even when the underlying density looks smooth and bell-shaped.

This is not only a really neat fact, it is also relevant in practice. In high-dimensional statistics and machine learning, the most likely point is often not a representative point. What matters is the typical set: the region where individual density may be lower, but where almost all the probability mass actually lives. That shows up in distance-based methods, anomaly detection, and Bayesian computation, where algorithms that chase the mode can miss the shell where ordinary samples are found.
