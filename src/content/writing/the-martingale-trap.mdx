The efficient market hypothesis is often stated too strongly by its defenders and too weakly by its critics.
It does not say that market prices are always correct, or that investors are always rational, or that bubbles are impossible.
The useful version is more modest:
**given the information available to you, it is very hard to predict future price changes in a way that earns excess returns.**

In its cleanest form, this is the martingale property.
Let $P_t$ be the price of an asset at time $t$, and let $\mathcal{F}_t$ represent the information available at that time.
Then a martingale satisfies:

$$
E[P_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = P_t.
$$

That is, conditional on what is currently known, tomorrow's expected price is today's price.
In practice, you would want to talk about discounted returns, dividends, risk premia, transaction costs, and other complications.
But the intuition is simple: if public information implied that an asset should obviously rise tomorrow, traders would buy it today, pushing up the price until the obvious profit disappeared.

This is a frustrating idea because markets constantly look inefficient.
Some asset looks too cheap.
Some spread looks too wide.
Some strategy produces small steady gains month after month.
The natural inference is that the market has made a mistake.
Often the better inference is that the trader has left out a state of the world.

The key distinction is between the usual outcome and the expected outcome.
People tend to reason from the usual outcome.
Markets, at least in competitive settings, are pulled toward the expectation.
And expectations are sensitive to rare events.

Suppose an asset pays $+1$ with probability 0.99 and $-100$ with probability 0.01.
Most of the time, holding this asset feels good.
If you looked at a short history, you would probably see a string of small gains.
The asset would appear to have a positive edge.
But its expected payoff is:

$$
0.99(1) + 0.01(-100) = -0.01.
$$

The rare bad state dominates the calculation.
If you ignore that state, the asset looks cheap.
If you include it, the cheapness disappears.

This is the structure behind "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller."
The pennies are visible.
The steamroller is a low-probability event, so it may not show up in the recent data.
But the price can still reflect it.
Indeed, this is exactly what an efficient market should do: price the bad state before it arrives.

This is a common source of false confidence among traders.
They find a trade that has worked for a while, infer that the market is inefficient, and then lever it up.
But the high Sharpe ratio may be a mirage created by censoring the bad part of the distribution.
A strategy can look calm not because it is safe, but because the risk is discontinuous.

The same point applies to assets that look cheap on simple valuation metrics.
A high yield, a low multiple, or a persistent spread is not automatically an inefficiency.
It may be a payment for bearing a risk that is hard to model, hard to hedge, or simply unpleasant to own.
The missing term in the spreadsheet is often not a small adjustment.
It is the state in which the asset is impaired, the financing disappears, or everyone tries to exit at once.

This does not prove that markets are perfectly efficient.
There are limits to arbitrage, institutional constraints, agency problems, taxes, mandates, and plain human error.
But it does suggest a useful prior:
when a trade looks obviously profitable, first assume that you have misunderstood the payoff distribution.

This is especially important because tail risks are easy to underweight.
The sample sizes are small, the historical record is incomplete, and the incentives are bad.
If a strategy earns small steady profits while occasionally blowing up, the person running it can look brilliant for years.
The market may look inefficient during the entire period in which the trader is being paid to hold the crash risk.
Then the crash arrives and the earlier profits are reinterpreted as compensation rather than alpha.

The martingale property is not a magic theorem that prevents people from making money.
It is a discipline for thinking clearly.
If you believe you have found an edge, ask what information you have that is not already in the price.
Then ask what rare event would make the trade lose a lot of money.
If the answer is "nothing," you probably have not thought hard enough.

The practical lesson is not to give up on investing or trading.
It is to be much less impressed by apparent inefficiencies.
Prices are not omniscient, but they are usually aggregating more information than you are.
And in many cases, the information you are missing is not subtle.
It is just sitting in the tail.
